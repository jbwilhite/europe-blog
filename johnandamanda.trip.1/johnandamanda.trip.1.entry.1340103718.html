Amsterdam, Noord-Holland, Netherlands
Tuesday, June 19, 2012

Amsterdam was the next stop on our list, and at 750,000 people or so, it is a fairly large city, but still more manageable than London and Paris. Amsterdam is famous for its liberal attitude towards drugs and prostitution, and even a walk through the Red Light District at 2 in the afternoon can be interesting. One thing you quickly learn in Amsterdam, a cafe is a bar/restaurant, but a coffee shop is where marijuana is sold. There is probably a coffee shop on every block.

We left early from Brugge, because we were worried we might have another train fiasco like last time, but everything went well. We have started picking up a little Dutch, but the words we know almost much exclusively apply to transportation. We have taken enough trains and trams to learn that: volgende halte = next stop, bestemming = ending, Maandag = Monday, Vridag = Friday, Zaterdag = Saturday, Zondag = Sunday (We have no idea what the other days of the week are, all the timetables list Maandag-Vridag times). Many Dutch words sound similar to the English counterparts, even if the spelling if significantly different. Maybe that is why everyone in Amsterdam spoke perfect English. Often they would just start out speaking English to us and wouldn't bother with the Dutch.

We arrived at the campground earlier than we were used to, and decided to just hang out at the campground restaurant/bar for the evening. The place started to fill up because of the Euro soccer tournament: Netherlands was playing Portugal, and Germany was playing Denmark. Pretty soon the place was packed with German and Dutch supporters. Netherlands ended up losing 2-1 to Portugal, an eliminating third loss for a team projected to be one of the favorites. The Dutch take their voetball very seriously, and were not at all pleased with their team's poor showing. Since Netherlands was eliminated, the next day we saw workers in the city pulling down the giant inflatable soccer balls and orange streamers that lined the streets.

Our campground was more like what I expected from our European camping experience. There were virtually no tents in Paris, and Brugge was nice but very quiet. There was a much younger and rowdier vibe to Amsterdam, which is as attributable to the city as anything else. The campground didn't seem to have quiet hours and somewhat catered to those who were enjoying Amsterdam's "liberties". While the other campgrounds were mostly camper vans and families, Camping Zeeburg was young people and tents. We were directed to set up anywhere in a large field full of tents. Plus there were goats and chickens and donkeys... no idea why.

The city of Amsterdam is fairly walkable, but we made use of the convenient tram system. The roads are set up so that there are two wide lanes for trams and buses, two narrow lanes outside that for cars, and outer narrow bike and scooter lanes. The car lanes were the least used; seems like everyone, young or old, rides a bike in Amsterdam. There several canals that circle the center of Amsterdam, and there are dozens of bridges over the canals. On every one of these bridges, there are dozens of bicycles chained or propped up. At first we thought they belonged to the people in the area, but we felt a few of the tires and figured out that they were abandoned. I guess when you get a new bike in Amsterdam, you just leave the old one wherever it happened to be.

Amsterdam is a very relaxed place, and we spent a good bit of our time just sitting at cafes and watching the people walk/bike by. It seems like all the cafes are full around 3 in the afternoon with locals having a few beers (does anyone work here?). So we joined in, had a beer, and laughed at the bicycles narrowly dodging the tourists (we like to think we blend in a little bit better: we don't wear fanny packs, tie our jackets around our waist, or wear shirts that say "Amsterdam"). One of the more interesting foods we tried was Dutch Croquettes, or bitterballen. They are some sort of beef and gravy that is breaded and fried. I'm not sure exactly what was in them, but they were good! We also stopped a a lunch counter place and had a couple Dutch sandwiches. I had half sliced liver and half salted beef, and Amanda had smoked eel, both were very different but delicious.

Though I'm a little ashamed to admit it, I was "hit" by a bike, but it was more like a graze. Funny thing though, it was in the campground and on the sidewalk, so I don't think I deserve any blame. A couple was renting bikes and getting the rundown on how to use them in the city, but they apparently weren't instructed on how to brake. These were fixed gear bikes with pedal brakes. After the girl bumped into me, the guy nearly ran into the back of her. So hopefully they learned how to brake before any real damage was done.

Overall we really enjoyed Amsterdam. We had a lot of fun just enjoying the laid-back atmosphere of the city. I'm sure there's great deal more experiences to be had in Amsterdam, but I guess we will have to come back! Next up, night train to Copenhagen!
