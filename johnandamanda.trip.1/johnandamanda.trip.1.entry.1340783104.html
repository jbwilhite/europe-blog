Vienna, Vienna, Austria
Wednesday, June 27, 2012

Vienna was a little different than I expected. I thought of Vienna as somewhat of a quaint old city with the sounds of Mozart and operas ringing in the streets. While the city center is very historical, Vienna (Wien in German) is a modern bustling city. Vienna is a smaller version of London, both are historical and modern, with plenty of small parks and tree lined streets. A busy city with around 1.5 million people, Vienna is very relaxed and livable for its size.

Vienna is Amanda's favorite city so far, and it is certainly up there on my list. We only had one full day here, but we felt like we saw a lot of what the city has to offer. The food was good, and still cheap compared to the rest of Europe. The city is not perfect though. The waiters are known for not being overly nice, but the people of Vienna seemed pretty friendly. The city allows smoking in restaurants and bars, which is almost extinct in the rest of Western Europe. Not only is it allowed, but pretty much everyone actually smokes in the restaurants.

Both places we went to eat were very good and reasonably priced. Neither had menus in English though. Amanda and I are adventurous eaters, so we stuck to the meals of the day and rolled the dice. For lunch, she could tell she was ordering spinach, so I assumed it was a salad. Her meal ended up being some sort of puréed spinach with hashbrowns and an egg on top. She's says its the weirdest meal she's ever eaten, but she enjoyed it. I thought was getting noodles, but it turns out that knödel is a potato dumpling. My meal was much more normal, and very delicious, stuffed pork tenderloin with potato dumplings. At dinner, we figured out that hühner is chicken and puten is turkey. Amanda had the huhnerschnitzel and I had puten natur mit champignon sauce und reis (natural turkey with mushroom sauce and rice).

We decided to trash our money belts in Vienna. Amanda had been just keeping hers in her purse, because it is too difficult to get money or cards out of it in public. I lost my debit card because it's just as easy to put the card between the money belt and my pants, as it is to put the card into the money belt pocket. I'm guessing it fell out of my pants leg in Copenhagen. I had a scare in Vienna because the snap came undone, and I thought the money belt (with my passport, credit cards and cash) was missing. Luckily my shorts held it in place on the side of my leg. They were only $4 on Amazon, so I guess we got what we paid for. We will just have to be careful with our pockets and her purse.

We went on a long looping walk through the center of the city, and north to Praterstern and the Danube. In town, we saw more grand old historic buildings than we can remember. Praterstern is a historical theme park sitting in a park on the northeast side of town. The ferris wheel was built in 1897 and is one of the oldest in the world. All our literature made references to an Orson Welles scene from the "famous" movie The Third Man shot here. I've never heard of this movie, so I don't see how it could be that famous. We also saw the Danube from Donauinsel (Danube Island). The island is a several kilometer long skinny artificial island built in the middle of the Danube (or maybe they expanded the river around this part of land). The island is basically one long park, and is popular with bikers and joggers. We heard that there is a nude beach on either end, but the walk was too far to investigate.

Vienna is a shopping city. There are tons of pedestrian streets with all the really expensive famous stores: Louis Vuitton, Gucci, Chanel, Tiffany and so on. On one of the less expensive streets, we found a cool graphic design and screen printing shop called Motmot (Design Website and Shop Website). We had some Tshirts printed, and talked to the owner while he was printing. He is originally from California and his girlfriend is from Italy, and they met while in college in California. She convinced him to move to Vienna and they opened up the shop. He said Vienna took some getting used to, but it's a good place to live. In one of the more touristy areas, we saw the most impressive street performers we seen so far. It is difficult to explain (check out the video at the bottom), but is basically an optical illusion to make one guy look like he is floating above the other. They must have some sort of elaborate hidden platform. They must've sat there, not moving, for over an hour, because we walked by several times.

We didn't have a whole lot of time for Vienna, but we really enjoyed what we did see. We still have a couple weeks in German speaking countries, so maybe we will be able to read a menu by the time we are done. Now we are leaving the big city life and heading to a tiny town called Hallstatt in the Salzkammergut (Salt Mining) region of Austria.
