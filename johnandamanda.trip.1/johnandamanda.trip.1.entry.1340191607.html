Copenhagen, Capital Region of Denmark, Denmark
Wednesday, June 20, 2012

Copenhagen is a city of contrasts: it is a historic city with some of the most modern buildings in the world, it is a major metropolitan area with a small city feel and it is a utilitarian port with artistic flair. Copenhagen has a population of over one million people, but the city center is so tight and organized, you would never know it. We thought we saw a lot of bikes in Amsterdam, but the people in Copenhagen live on their bikes. I read somewhere that 70% of the citizens ride a bike daily, but it seems like more. The increase of bikes and decrease of cars make the city center feel less congested and more enjoyable.

With some apprehension, we reserved our beds in a 6 bed couchette on the night train to Copenhagen on our first day in Amsterdam. On the day of departure, we got lucky. We arrived at the station an hour early, and while waiting at the platform, we were told the train departure had been moved to Utrecht (30 minutes southeast). If we had not been early, we might not have made the train. We knew our cabin would be tiny, but it was still surprisingly small (the pictures don't do it justice). We were hoping that the transfer to Utrecht meant we might have less people in the cabin, but the other four people made it too.

We got lucky with our bunk mates; there was a young Swiss couple, a young Australian guy, and a middle aged Australian lady (the two Aussies didn't know each other). The Swiss couple spoke perfect English, so we all talked for a few hours before sleeping. Everyone was nice, and they didn't seem like they would rummage through our stuff in the middle of the night. We spent a good bit of time with them watching Holland and Germany go by in the hallway outside our cabin. I guess nobody wanted to spend the entire 15 hours cooped up in a bunk that we couldn't even sit up in.

When we got to Copenhagen, we took a regional train straight to our campground (this campground was the furthest from a city center yet - about 7km). The walk from the station to the campground took us through a park with a big historical house and the Aquarium of Denmark. The campground was situated in the middle of a beach park that got very crowded during the day. The area was a destination in itself, and we spent the rest of the day exploring the surrounding beach and parks.

The campground was, by far, the nicest and most interesting so far (and the most expensive). The camping area was set up inside a former fort that protected Copenhagen's harbor from 1887 to 1934. The bunkers and cannons were still there. One of the bunkers was converted to the kitchen/lounge/bathrooms area, but was designed in an industrial modern way. The Danish are big on architecture and interior design, and we saw countless nice buildings and spaces. Ironically we were camping in a fort, but it was the least secure place we have been to yet. It seems like many people figured out that the shortest way to the beach was through the campground, so there was a lot of foot traffic by our tent during the day. But everything was fine. I talked to a few of the people, and the Danes seem very friendly.

One thing we have noticed in Europe is increased efforts towards energy conservation. Utility costs are much higher over here and it shows. Most lights, especially in the bathrooms, are on a motion sensor. Some sinks in the US have the knobs you have to push down for the water to run, then cut off after 15 seconds or so. Here, the showers are either like that, or require coins for water. Either way, it annoys you into speeding up the process.

In all honesty, the weather so far has been not been great. Every city has been colder than usual, but it seems like it clears up right after we leave. There has been a cold and rainy patch of weather moving at about our same speed and direction. So it was surprising that we had some sun when we arrived in Copenhagen. But it didn't last; the bad weather caught back up with us on our last day here, and it poured. Next up is Prague, a good ways south, so we should finally escape this stubborn weather system. Though I'm sure we will miss it when we are sweating in Italy.
