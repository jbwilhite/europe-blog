Hallstatt, Upper Austria, Austria
Friday, June 29, 2012

The village of Hallstatt, Austria is the epitome of a postcard town. Sandwiched between mountains and the lake, the town is situated on one narrow street, with a few cobbled lanes branching off. Every view is either a breathtaking mountain vista or picturesque little Austrian houses perched on the mountainside. All the houses have immaculate flower window boxes and tiny flower gardens. Amanda described Hallstatt as "the cutest town in the world," and she is probably right.

Our last two stops were cities with well over a million people, but Hallstatt has less than a thousand permanent residents. I'm sure that number is much higher in the summer because of all the tourists. Hallstatt has no hotels, only gasthofs (guesthouses), so it seems to be more if a day trip for the tour buses. From 10am to 6pm the town swells with the elderly and Chinese/Japanese tourists (the two main groups who like to travel in packs and use tour bus groups). The Chinese love Hallstatt so much, they have cloned it! Click here for more info.

We thought Hallstatt was small enough to not have too many tourists, but the crowds were pretty easy to avoid. We spent the middle of the day exploring the outskirts and surrounding attractions. Around dinner time, we felt like we had Hallstatt to ourselves. The two semifinal matches of the European Cup soccer tournament took place on the two nights we were there. There are only a couple of bars in town, and we watched the matches with locals. The second match was Germany vs Italy, and it became quickly apparent that Austrians were pulling for Italy. We watched the second half back at the campground with several Germans. I struck up a conversation with an older German couple, and even though they spoke very little English and I speak even less German, we had a pretty good conversation. They are from somewhere near Munich, and I told them I was from America östen and süden (east and south). They have some some sort of connection to Atlanta (family maybe?). I told them we were pulling for Deutschland because we are going to be in Munich on July 1st, the day of the final. They also asked if my father or grandfather was Deutsch, because of my hair and eyes. I told them it went further back than that, but I do have German heritage.

The most notable activity we did in Hallstatt was a tour of the salt mines. Hallstatt's salt mine is way up the mountain above the city. We had to ride a funicular to get there. The mountains around Salzburg (Literally Salt City), are full of salt deposits. The salt mine at Hallstatt has been active for over 4000 years. The Hallstatt salt trade was so important in the prehistoric times that a period of the Bronze Age is called the Hallstatt era. The town and mine have been declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site because of the history associated with Hallstatt. The tour even included wooden slides that the miners used to get to certain areas quicker (video at the bottom).

One problem we had in Hallstatt and several other places, is the difficulty getting tap water with a meal. We are on a tight budget, so we can't afford to be spending €3 every meal on a Coke or bottle of mineral water. Most places will begrudgingly bring you a tiny glass of water if you ask, but some just refuse. I learned that leitungswasser means tap water to try and better get my point across, but it is still difficult. We always carry bottles if water around, so it's not a big deal to get no drink with a meal. We are not sure how anyone in Europe stays hydrated if they never drink water.

Hallstatt was a great start to our break from cities. As we go further south, the less major cities, and the more small towns we are visiting. Munich, Florence, and Rome are the only big cities left on the itinerary. Salzburg, next up, is fairly large at 150k, but still a small and manageable city.
